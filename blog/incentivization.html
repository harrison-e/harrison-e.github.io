12 April 2024

The Proper Incentivization of Competition

Azure waves crash against a rocky shore, frothing at the mouth of a remote coastline. Seafoam blooms into a strong, briny aroma that meets the nose of a young explorer. A gentleman, an unfinished naturalist, with a keen interest in living creatures, and their surroundings. As the interloper navigates the maze of damp stones before him, he glimpses a black blur, swooping through his peripheral vision.

His head quickly turns to trace the path of motion, and he eventually spots a black speck on a distant branch. He's nearly certain he's seen this creature before--days ago, on a neighboring landmass. However, this exotic archipelago has already proven to the curious man that his paradigm of the animal kingdom was grossly incomplete. So, after fumbling through his satchel, he finds his binoculars, in an attempt to validate his hypothesis.

Carefully calibrating the magnification, he peers through the lenses of his binoculars, only to have his mind's assumption refuted by what he observes. The beak of this finch is remarkably different from that of last week, sparking confusion. This strange land of craters continues to surprise him.

Years later, as a more mature Darwin meditates on his travels, he develops his fundamental theory of evolution: that living things well-suited to their environment will pass their traits on, and those that aren't won't. Essentially, this theory frames the living world as a competition. Every living thing is vying for the limited resources of their environment, and over time, a genetic code is optimized for a dynamic set of circumstances.

This hypothesis has been wildly successful in framing our natural world. However, I believe that its ideas have yet to be fully interpolated into our framing of the uniquely human world. In the near two centuries that have passed since the publishing of "On the Origin of Species", although our physical environments have experienced rapid, significant changes, our conceptual environment remains chained to Darwin's thesis. Whether consciously or not, we humans are still voraciously and destructively competing for resources, even in an environment where many forms of such aggression are not conducive to the long-term success of our species.

A few key traits have enabled humanity's ascension to the top of the food chain on Earth. Among them disposable thumbs, bipedal locomotion, and most importantly, language. Our brains have evolved to hold dictionaries, keyed on words and sounds and valued on concepts and ideas. But why have we evolved such rich and complex forms of communication?

Of course, the answer to this question is tacit--we have evolved language to effectively communicate with one another. Fundamentally, our path to world dominance is only possible through our dependency on one another. Along this path, however, our species has withstood repeated infighting. As we conquer the rest of the living world, our competition has shifted. No longer are we truly competing with the likes of tigers and bears, but now with one another. Sharks, snakes, and lions costumed in human flesh. Although our food is abundant, many humans around the world starve.

But how efficient is it for an apex predator with such ubiquitous power and control to compete in such ways? Clearly, our combined efforts have vastly outpaced the natural processes of optimization. In fact, we've mastered the concept of optimization so completely that we've convinced rocks to optimize our problems for us!

The main resource in our current meta-environment as humans has been principally abstracted as capital. With capital, some humans can influence change in this environment much more effectively than those without. Thus, by our nature of conquest, we have begun to optimize for capital. While this form of society has created an environment for great strides in scientific understanding, and thus quality of life for humanity, it has been equally detrimental to the prolonged success of mankind. If we are to secure a future for our posterity, it is far wiser to optimize for a more inclusive, comprehensive goal. After all, the success of one human is dependent on the success of all humans.

The optimization for capital has in many cases, though most notably regarding the health of our natural world, bred disadvantageous consequences for both humanity at large, and the natural world upon which we are precariously seated. Through maximizing capital profit margins, monolithic corporate entities have relentlessly chipped away at the foundations of our species. The loss of countless self-balancing natural cycles in favor of cheaper, less futureproof alternatives has taken place under this regime.

In a hyperstimulated society, where goady hedonism is hypnotized into the fabric of our personalities, and our isolated lifestyles disconnect us from one another, it is easy to forget what has fueled our meteoric rise to the throne. However, at a time where we are at our most capable, and where one human with Internet access can effectively tap into the entire reservoir of past and present human knowledge and understanding, we cannot forget this. Collaboration is our greatest strength, and our most valuable resource. It is the reason we have persevered through near-extinction events. It is the reason we so deeply and intrinsically crave connection. And it is what will divert us from a path of ultimate self-destruction. Let us compete not for the resources that are so abundant, but for the overall success of a species that depends so heavily upon its environment.
